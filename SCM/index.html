How It Works (Using 6 Business Cases)

There are six interdependent business entities in a supply chain:

  • Supplier: Sources raw materials such as cotton or synthetic fibers, ensuring good quality and availability.
  • Manufacturer: Converts raw material into finished goods through processes such as spinning, dyeing, and weaving.
  • Distributor: Acts as a bridge, transporting products from manufacturers to wholesalers efficiently.
  • Wholesaler: Manages bulk inventory and ensures availability for retailers.
  • Retailer: Directly interacts with consumers, providing products based on market demand.
  • Consumer: The end-user whose satisfaction and feedback influence the entire chain.

These entities are interdependent, and any disruption at one level affects the whole system. Algorithms such as predictive analytics, optimization techniques, and logistics models are applied to address challenges like inventory management, demand forecasting, and waste reduction.

Why It Is Necessary for a Smart City Project

  • Sustainability: Optimized supply chains reduce waste and carbon footprints, aligning with eco-friendly initiatives.
  • Smart Logistics: Advanced algorithms enable real-time tracking, route optimization, and faster delivery, crucial for urban living.
  • Cost Efficiency: By reducing delays and inventory mismanagement, SCM enhances the economic viability of smart city operations.
  • Consumer-Centric Approach: Smart cities prioritize citizens' needs, and a robust SCM ensures the availability of goods and services at the right time and place.

This project aligns with the goals of smart cities and shows how technology and algorithms can transform the processes of supply chains to meet future urban challenges.
